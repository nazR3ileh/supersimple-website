GLASGOW COMA SCALE(GCS)

A neurological assessment tool called the Glasgow Coma Scale (GCS) is used to gauge a person's level of consciousness after suffering a brain injury or other neurological problems. Three factors are assessed: verbal reaction, physical response, and eye opening. There is a maximum score of 15 with a range of 1 to 4, 5 or 6 for each component. The severity of the brain damage or disability increases with decreasing score. The GCS assists medical practitioners in assessing a patient's condition severity and directing suitable treatment regimens. The Scale was introduced in 1974 by Graham Teasdale and Bryan Jennett.

WHEN TO USE GLASGOW COMA SCALE?

Most patients in the emergency room (ER) or intensive care unit (ICU) who require a GCS assessment have experienced traumatic brain damage. Until the medical staff indicates otherwise, a GCS should be performed at the time of admission and then every four hours.

SCORING OF GLASGOW COMA SCALE:

The highest possible score is 15 which is fully conscious and the lowest possible score is 3 which is considered coma or dead.

Both the individual components and the total score hold significance. Both a numerical representation of each component of a patient's GCS (such as E3V5M5) and an addition of the separate components to obtain a total Coma Score (such as E3V5M5 = 13) are possible. As an illustration, a score may be written as GCS 13 = E3 V5 M5.

Severe: GCS 3-8

Moderate: GCS 9-12

Mild: GCS 13-15

FOUR STEPS TO THE ASSESSMENT PROCESS:

1.) CHECK. For factors interfering with communication, ability to respond and other injuries.

2.) OBSERVE. Eye opening, content of speech and movements of left and right sides.

3.) STIMULATE. Sound: Spoken or shouted request; Physical: Pressure on finger tip, Trapezius or Supraorbital Notch.

4.) RATE. Assign according to highest response observed.
